Thus, bacteria exit the ruminant\ngastrointestinal tract and persist on contaminated food matter\nbefore being taken up into a human host, where they colonize\nand cause diarrheal disease. After human uptake, bacteria are\nexposed to a range of host-specific cues, including a shift in\ntemperature, passage through the acidic stomach environment,\nneutralization through bicarbonate exposure, and finally, the in-\ntestinal environment. It has always been assumed that sequen-\ntial exposure to these host-specific triggers is sufficient to induce\nvirulence exclusively within the human host niche, the intestine.\nPrevious studies have indeed demonstrated induction of Ler,\nand thus LEE, in response to environmental stimuli. For exam-\nple, GrlA is expressed in response to bicarbonate released by\nthe pancreas, which partially induces LEE, and thus virulence\n(22, 23). Here, we show that although the levels of GrlA have\na subtle effect on Ler activation, full virulence induction is\nonly achieved through host attachment. This departs from our\nprevious understanding of GrlA-based regulation, which was\nthought to require GrlR for inhibition and release of GrlA from\nthe GrlR complex to achieve activation. In contrast to this, our\ndata give strong evidence supporting the hypothesis that full\ninduction by GrlA relies on mechanically stimulated activation\nof free GrlA, whereas the same cue does not activate GrlR-\nbound GrlA. How exactly GrlA becomes competent to bind to\nor activate the LEE1 promoter is clearly more complex than a\ntransition from GrlR-bound to unbound states. It could be a\nresult of a change in subcellular localization, posttranslational\nmodification, or additional binding partners; these possibilities\nwill be addressed in future work.
